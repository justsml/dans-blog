## Article Summary
This analytical and cautionary article refutes the common management practice of adding developers to late software projects, demonstrating through the quadratic communication formula n(n-1)/2 that team expansion exponentially increases coordination overhead and onboarding friction. Grounded in empirical research, it explains how modern engineering practices like CI/CD pipelines and microservices reduce but cannot escape the fundamental O(n²) math of human context-switching and mentorship costs. Framed around recurring mathematical and biological metaphors—such as the “coordination tax,” “mentorship tax,” and Brooks’ “nine women can’t make a baby in one month” analogy—the piece targets engineering managers and technical leaders, advocating for smaller, autonomous teams of 5–8 people to preserve velocity and code quality.
