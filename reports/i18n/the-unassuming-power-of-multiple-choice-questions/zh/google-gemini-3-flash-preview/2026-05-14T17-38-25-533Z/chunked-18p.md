## Article Summary
This analytical article argues that multiple-choice questions (MCQs) are a sophisticated pedagogical tool rather than a mere assessment shortcut, provided the "distractors" (wrong answers) are designed to represent specific mental models or common misconceptions. The tone is reflective and instructional, framing the creation of MCQs as a "test of the explanation itself" that requires the author to respect and anticipate the learner's likely errors. Ultimately, the piece posits that the true value of the format lies in its ability to reveal the "edges" of a concept through intentional, believable wrong answers.
